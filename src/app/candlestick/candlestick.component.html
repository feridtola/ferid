Candlestick Pattern

In financial technical analysis, a candlestick pattern is a movement in prices shown graphically on a candlestick chart. Candlestick patterns are used to predict the future direction of price movement. Discover 4 of the most common candlestick patterns and how you can use them to identify trading opportunities.

Let’s dive in...

Bullish Candlestick Pattern

Bullish patterns may form after a market downtrend, and signal a reversal of price movement. They are an indicator for traders to consider opening a long position to profit from any upward trajectory.

1. Hammer
A hammer is a bullish reversal pattern that forms after a price decline.
Here’s how to recognize it:
  • Little to no upper shadow
  • The price closes at the top 1⁄4 of the range.
  • The lower shadow is about two or three times as long as the body.
And that means a hammer:
  • When the market opens, the sellers take control and lower the price.
  • At the height of the sale, there is enormous buying pressure and pushes the price up.
  • The buying pressure is so strong that it closes above the opening price.
2. Bullish Engulfing Pattern
A Bullish Engulfing Pattern is a (2-candle) bullish reversal candlestick pattern that forms after a decline in price.
Here’s how to recognize it:
  • The first candle has a bearish ending.
  • The body of the second candle completely "covers" the body of the first candle.
  • The second candle closes bullishly.
And this is what a bullish engulfing pattern means:
  • At the first candle, sellers are in control as they have closed a low price for the period.
  • The second candle is under strong buying pressure and therefore closes above the high of the previous candle - which shows that buyers have won the battle for the time being.

Bearish Candlestick Pattern

Bearish candlestick patterns usually form after an uptrend, and signal a point of resistance. Heavy pessimism about the market price often causes traders to close their long positions, and open a short position to take advantage of the falling price.

1. Shooting Star
A Shooting Star is a (1- candle) bearish reversal pattern that forms after an advanced in price.
Here’s how to recognize it:
  • Little to no lower shade.
  • The price closes at the bottom 1⁄4 of the range.
  • The upper shadow is about two or three times as long as the body.
And this is what a shooting star means:
  • When the market opened, buyers took control and pushed the price up.
  • At the height of the purchase, there is tremendous selling pressure, pushing the price down.
  • The selling pressure is so strong that it closes below the opening price.
2. Bearish Engulfing Pattern
A Bearish Engulfing Pattern is a (2-candle) bearish reversal candlestick pattern that forms after an advanced in price.
Here’s how to recognize it:
  • The first candle has a bullish ending.
  • The body of the second candle completely "covers" the body of the first candle.
  • The second candle closes bearishly.
And this is what a bearish engulfing pattern means:
  • With the first candle, buyers are in control, as they could close a higher price for the period.
  • There is strong selling pressure on the second candle and closes below the low of the previous candle - which shows that the sellers have won the battle for the time being.
